ABSTRACT
Many software systems utilize forums to allow a broad set
of stakeholders to request features. However the resulting
mass of ideas and comments can make prioritization and
management of feature requests challenging. In this paper
we propose a novel approach for partially automating the
creation of personas from a set of feature requests in open
forums. Our approach utilizes topic clustering, classica-
tion, and association rules to identify meaningful groupings
of feature requests and then uses them to guide the construc-
tion of personas. Once created, these personas are leveraged
to coordinate feature requests, track changes, and to pro-
vide stakeholder communication mechanisms. We illustrate
our approach with examples taken from the health-insurance
domain and then evaluate it against feature requests in the
SugarCRM project.

1. INTRODUCTION
All software systems must satisfy a wide range of func-
tional and quality-focused concerns [3]. These concerns are
often elicited from stakeholders using online discussion fo-
rums. While this enables a relatively inclusive elicitation
process it also introduces a range of problems such as re-
dundant requests, topics dispersed widely across multiple
discussion threads, and unanswered posts [6]. Such problems
can only partially be mitigated through current practices of
using search technologies to recommend relevant threads for
new posts [6].

2. PERSONAS
Personas were introduced to user interaction design by
Alan Cooper in the mid 1990s through his now infamous
book \The inmates are running the Asylum" [8]. Each per-
sona is given a name and described in terms of psyche, back-
ground, emotions and attitudes. Personas are traditionally
created through a fairly rigorous series of user analysis ac-
tivities which include surveys and interviews, slicing of users
into categories, data collection and analysis to test whether
candidate slices create distinguishable user groups, and anal-
ysis of potential usage patterns within each group.
In our prior work, we demonstrated how personas could
be used to support the architectural design process [4, 5]
and argued that using Architecturally Signicant (AS) Per-
sonas as an aid to understanding architectural concerns in
early phases of a project could guide the architectural design
process and minimize the need for late-stage costly refactor-
ings. Our AS-personas are therefore designed to drive ar-
chitectural and software design as opposed to user-interface
design. In this paper we present a novel, semi-automated
approach for extracting feature requests from online forums,
and then using this information to generate candidate per-
sonas.

3. PERSONA CREATION
To represent the diverse interests of real stakeholders, per-
sonas need to be multi-faceted. We generate such personas
in the four-step process of (1) clustering individual posts
into topics, (2) performing association rule mining to dis-
cover groupings of topics which tend to be of interest to
multiple stakeholders, (3) identifying quality concerns for
each grouping of topics related to attributes such as per-
formance, security, and availability, and nally (4) recom-
mending groups of topics from which to create individual
personas, and providing supporting information about asso-
ciated feature requests from which an analyst can create a
persona. We discuss each of these steps below.3.1 Topic Identiï¬cation
Topic identication is accomplished through clustering fea-
ture requests. Unfortunately forum data is very `noisy.' In
addition to the targeted feature requests the posts include
complaints, calls for help, social comments (e.g. \Nice to
hear from you again"), and user's names. They also in-
clude foreign language terms, typos, and spelling errors.
Within this challenging context, our goal is to identify fea-
tures of interest to the users. For example, a feature re-
quest taken from the OpenEMR (Electrical Medical Record)
system states that \Just wanted to know, Is there any way
to transfer the whole billed amount to patient responsibility,
when the insurance denies the claims? One way to post it
through batch payments, but it's too lengthy."
As topics tend to be dispersed across posts in multiple
discussion threads, we take individual posts as the primitive
unit for clustering purposes. Each of the generated clus-
ters represents a single topic. We utilized the Incremental
Diusive Clustering (IDC) algorithm to perform this clus-
tering because it has been shown to produce quite accurate
and meaningful clusters of features. It places each post into
one or more cluster according to the number of its underly-
ing topics. For example, the example feature request could
conceivably cluster into two separate topics related to claim
denial and billing . Each topic is named automatically by
identifying the medoid of its cluster, dened as the descrip-
tor that is most representative of the cluster's theme. Due
to space constraints we do not formaly dene the IDC algo-
rithm here; however a complete description is provided by
Hariri et al. [9].
3.2 Association rule mining
Creating one persona per topic would lead to a prolifer-
ation of personas. We therefore use association rule min-
ing to infer meaningful groupings of topics [10]. For exam-
ple, we might discover that users interested in requests for
quotes also tended to be interested in reviewing and compar-
ing quotes, or in information privacy . This idea is illustrated
in Figure 3. We utilize association rule mining to infer these
relationships.

3.4 Recommending Personas
The combined use of topic analysis, association rule min-
ing, and quality-concern classication produces a set of re-
lated topics, each of which is dened in terms of featurerequests and quality concerns. These \megatopics" serve as
the basis of our persona recommendation process.
For each recommended persona, we rst present a set of
topics to the user representing the groupings identied using
association rule mining. The user can accept or reject topics
for several reasons including (i) the user doesn't believe that
the set of topics are suciently related, or (ii) a topic was
formed around a non-feature. Examples of the second case
include topics formulated around unusual user's names or
terms such as help orrunwhich might be commonly used
in the forum but not removed as stopwords.
For each accepted topic, the user is presented with a list of
related posts. She can search through these posts to identify
sentences or phrases to assign to the persona as user stories
or can write her own stories that reect the stakeholders'
posts. We considered, but rejected the idea of automati-
cally mining the most representative feature requests from
the forum and assigning these directly to the stakeholder in
favor of a manual requirements (user story) extraction phase
guided by the carefully clustered posts. This approach facil-
itated high quality, comprehensible, focused personas, which
better supported our end goal.

We demonstrate and evaluate our approach against fea-
ture requests in the SugarCRM system. SugarCRM is a pop-
ular open-source, customer relationship management system
designed to automate sales, customer services, and mar-
keting processes [1]. We selected SugarCRM because it is
used in both open source and commercial open source ap-
plications utilized by stakeholders in a broad set of areas,
including business, healthcare, technology and transporta-
tion. The broad interests of the stakeholders, coupled with
481an active customer base results in copious requests for new
features.

C, the Apriori algorithm attempts to nd the sets of top-

4.3 Evaluating Personas
To evaluate our approach we formulated the research ques-
tion \Are the personas assigned (i.e. registered for) to each
stakeholder appropriate and complete?" To answer this ques-
tion we took a random sampling of 25 stakeholders from the
forum. We eliminated any posts which were not feature re-
quests { for example, comments such as \Yes I like that idea
too," or \Can anyone tell me how to do X?" For stakeholders
with multiple posts related to several dierent topics, we se-
lected the rst instance of each topic. A persona assignment
was deemed correct if the body of the user's post contained
a reference to a topic of the stakeholder's interest. The two
researchers manually inspected each of the posts and identi-
ed the relevant persona(s). A complete listing of the posts,
the assigned stakeholders, and the evaluation results are pro-
vided online at http://re.cs.depaul.edu/Artifacts/Personas.
4.3.1 Results
Based on our analysis of the complete sample of 25 per-
sonas, 13 stakeholders had completely correct personas as-
signed while 7 stakeholders had exactly one relevant per-
sona missing. Of these, 4 were correctly assigned 1 persona
(while missing 1) and 3 were correctly assigned 2 personas
(while missing 1). There were no cases of incorrect assign-
ments; however, we expect that this class of error would
occur if we were to create ner grained clusterings and more
focused personas. Finally we had 5 instances of missing
topics. These topics include synchronization, calender and
reminders, shortcuts for ltering purposes, and a request for
tagging of open SugarCRM tabs. An analysis of these miss-
ing topics showed that there were insucient related posts
in the forum for them to be identied as major topics.

5. THREATS TO V ALIDITY
Evaluating new processes in Software Engineering can be
challenging { primarily because demonstrating the viability
of a new software engineering process or infrastructure, such
as the one presented here, requires using the new approach
in a real project during the actual lifetime of the project.
This is out of scope for the current work. However, we have
demonstrated the viability of using data mining techniques
to identify realistic groupings of topics and associated cross-
cutting quality concerns, and then using these results to cre-
ate representative, useful personas. Furthermore, our evalu-
ation is limited to a single open-source project and evaluates
only whether the personas created in response to the auto-
483mated recommendations provided sucient coverage to the
stakeholders. While this assessment was made by two mem-
bers of this team, thereby introducing potential bias, we
mitigated this risk by clearly dening the criterion for asso-
ciating a persona with a stakeholder. Furthermore, we have
publicly posted our evaluation results for review by others.
6. RELATED WORK
Several researchers have developed techniques for extract-
ing requirements from online forum data. For example,
Scacchi describes techniques for using online forums to gather
requirements [14] while Noll introduces online forums as a
way to elicit the requirements for open source software[13].
Several authors have proposed the use of data mining algo-
rithms for identifying personas. Sinha used Principal Com-
ponent Analysis (PCA) to discover dissimilarities among in-
dividuals through identifying the most discriminant factors
in data [15]. Tu et al used PCA to remove insignicant
questions in a survey and then Clustering Analysis(CA) to
group similar users based on their answers. They create one
persona per group by interviewing users [16]. Miaskiewicz et
aluse Latent Semantic Analysis(LSA) in order to discover
the similarities between responses of interviewees to a set
of questions[12]. These approaches dier from ours because
their intent is to create personas early in the development
process based on answers to survey questions in order to
guide the user interaction design of the system. In contrast,
our approach develops personas for the purpose of managing
and communicating information in a forum. Furthermore,
their approaches are limited to text clustering, while ours in-
troduces the additional step of association rule mining. This
means that very disparate topics { which happen to be of
interest to similar stakeholders could be grouped together.
There have also been several attempts to extract require-
ments knowledge from open source forums. Huang et al pro-
posed an approach for extracting <thread-title, reply> pairs
as chat knowledge using a Support Vector Machine(SVM)
[11]. Vlas and Robinson applied a rule-based natural lan-
guage classier to extract feature requests [2]. Although,
the nature of data is similar, their purpose is to extract re-

7. CONCLUSION
In this paper we have proposed a novel approach that
mines feature requests from forums, identies cohesive top-
ics, learns associations between those topics based on inter-
ests of actual users, identies cross-cutting concerns, and
uses this information to recommend personas. These per-
sonas can be used to anchor the feature management pro-
cess in order to tame the chaos that can pervade large open
feature-request forums.
We have described the application of our approach to over
six thousand feature requests mined from the SugarCRM fo-
rum, and demonstrated the viability of generating a useful
set of personas for managing feature requests. Future work
will explore the use of such personas in active projects across
a far broader set of domains and will also explore the usabil-
ity aspects connected to the task of persona creation.
